Reflection on 'Handmade Web' by J.R. Carpenter

The concept of the 'handmade web,' as articulated by J.R. Carpenter, evokes a sense of nostalgia and resistance. It takes us back to a time when the internet was more personal, intimate, and a true reflection of individual creativity. Carpenter's narrative draws a poignant contrast between the early web, characterized by its amateur aesthetics and DIY ethos, and the current corporate-dominated internet landscape.

What strikes me most is the notion that the handmade web is more than just a relic of the past; it is an act of defiance. In an age where web pages are predominantly generated by sophisticated tools and algorithms, Carpenter's advocacy for hand-coding web pages resonates as a call to reclaim a sense of agency and authenticity in our digital expressions. This is particularly relevant in today's world, where our online identities are often shaped and constrained by corporate platforms.

"The more proprietary, predatory, and puerile a place the web becomes, the more committed I am to using it in poetic and intransigent ways."

Carpenter's reflection also highlights the loss of direct interaction with the web's underlying code, particularly as mobile devices and tablets increasingly dominate our browsing experiences. This loss is not just a technical one but also a cultural and creative diminution, as fewer people have the opportunity to engage with the web as a malleable medium that they can shape and personalize.

Ultimately, this essay is a reminder of the power and potential of the web as a platform for individual expression. It encourages us to consider how we might bring some of the ethos of the 'handmade web' into our modern digital practices. Whether through hand-coding a simple page, engaging with creative web projects, or simply taking the time to appreciate the artistry of a well-crafted site, there is much to be gained from revisiting the values of the early web.
